Embassy & Travel Support


If something happens in the field or while abroad, your embassy / consulate can help you find safe options and navigate local systems.


Use this page to register travel, locate your nearest mission, and access context-specific safety resources.

Disclaimer: The resources provided on this website are not exhaustive or definitive. Regulations, reporting protocols, and legal requirements may vary by location and institution. Before conducting fieldwork, confirm current information with your university legal services, ethics or Title IX office, or a qualified legal professional.

Register Your Travel

  • Enroll your trip with your government’s traveler registry so officials can reach you during emergencies and you can receive alerts.
  • Share your itinerary (lodging, local contacts, transit) with your supervisor and trusted peers.
  • Store backups of passport, visas, insurance, and key phone numbers (printed + offline digital).

How can your embassy or consulate help?

Embassies and consulates exist to assist their citizens when problems arise abroad. While they cannot intervene in local investigations or legal proceedings, they can offer critical support such as:

  • Helping you replace a lost or stolen passport
  • Connecting you with local medical care, legal aid, and translation services
  • Assisting in contacting family, friends, or your home institution in emergencies
  • Providing lists of local attorneys and hospitals
  • Offering information on local laws and customs that may affect your safety
  • Coordinating with local authorities if you are detained, hospitalized, or a victim of crime

If you are conducting fieldwork, it’s recommended to register your travel plans with your country’s travel registry program so your embassy can reach you in the event of a natural disaster, political unrest, or emergency evacuation.

Reporting to Your Embassy or Consulate

Consular staff can explain local procedures and connect you with resources. They can coordinate with police if you want to report and help with practical needs while you make decisions.

  • They can: link you to medical care; help replace a lost or stolen passport; contact family or your employer (with your consent); provide lists of lawyers; explain possible financial assistance to return to your country; and request updates from local authorities on your case; file a report on your behalf after you have safely left the country.
  • They cannot: investigate crimes, give legal advice or represent you, serve as official interpreters, or pay legal or medical costs.
  • How to find yours: select your country of citizenship from the dropdown regions above, and use the “Find My Local Embassy” link to open the directory for the country you’re currently in.

Information here is general and may change or be inaccurate depending on your country of citizenship—confirm details with the local embassy or consulate before you act.
