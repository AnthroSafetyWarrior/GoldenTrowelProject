Reporting Misconduct and Safety Incidents


Reporting is how you document harm, get support, and help prevent future incidents. This page walks you through who to contact, how to record what happened, and what to expect—whether you’re in the field, abroad, or back at your home institution.

Disclaimer: The resources provided on this website are not exhaustive or definitive. Regulations, reporting protocols, and legal requirements may vary by location and institution. Before conducting fieldwork, confirm current information with your university legal services, ethics or Title IX office, or a qualified legal professional.

Your health and safety comes first.

If you’re in danger or need urgent care, act now. You control what you share and when. Advocates can help you stay safe, protect your academic standing, and decide if/when to report.

Do this first (as needed):

  • Get to safety / call emergency services. If abroad, contact your embassy or consulate for urgent assistance and safe transport.
  • Seek medical care. Hospitals/clinics can treat injuries, provide STI/HIV prophylaxis and emergency contraception, and—where available—offer forensic exams.
    Tip: If possible, bring clothing in a paper bag; avoid washing if you may want evidence collected. You can decline any step.
  • Execute your Fieldwork Safety Plan. If you're feeling overwhelmed, contact your emergency advocate and follow the steps outlined in your plan. You can also ask your designated emergency advocate or team contact to reach out to others on your behalf—sometimes that makes getting help easier and less overwhelming.
    • For an actionable checklist, access the Emergency Procedure Checklist
  • Connect with an advocate or Title IX professional. If you’d prefer not to enact your Fieldwork Safety Plan, your institution’s Title IX or Ethics office includes trained professionals who can connect you with confidential advocates, safety planning, and supportive resources. They can also contact others or coordinate accommodations for you if that helps lessen the burden. Reporting remains your choice.

Your choices, your pace

You can seek medical care and support without filing a report. Document what you can (dates, times, locations, messages) only if it feels safe.

  • Ask for safety accommodations (travel changes, extensions, housing).
  • Bring a trusted person or advocate to any appointment.
  • You can stop or say no at any time.

When you’re ready to consider reporting, continue to the resources below.

Documentation is Key

Write down everything you remember as soon as you can—what happened, when, where, and who was present. Even small details can matter later.


If you have texts, emails, photos, or recordings, save and back them up in a secure place. When possible, share a copy with a trusted advocate, advisor, or Title IX contact so the record is protected even if your devices are lost or compromised.


You don’t have to decide about reporting now—documentation simply preserves your options.

Reporting to Local Law Enforcement

You can choose whether to report. Laws and procedures vary by state, but many areas use Sexual Assault Response Teams (SARTs) that coordinate medical care, forensic exams, and police response. You can ask police to notify your embassy or consulate for support.

  • If you want to report: Try to report as soon as possible so evidence can be preserved. If you change clothes, keep what you were wearing in paper (not plastic). Save texts or messages and tell police if you think you were drugged. Ask for a copy or case number of the police report.
  • Forensic exam (“rape kit”): You may have an exam to preserve DNA and receive medical care. You do not have to report to get an exam, and you should not be billed for it. Minors may trigger mandatory reporting.
  • What happens next: Investigations and charging decisions are handled by prosecutors and may continue even if you later choose not to participate, depending on local law. Statutes of limitations for filing charges vary by state.

Procedures may differ depending on the country where you conduct research. Visit the Know Your Rights page, select your research country, and click the link under the “Reporting to Local Authorities” column for details.

If you need confidential support or help locating an exam site, contact the National Sexual Assault Hotline (RAINN): 800-656-HOPE (4673) or chat online at hotline.rainn.org.

Reporting to Your Embassy or Consulate

Consular staff can explain local procedures and connect you with resources. They can coordinate with police if you want to report and help with practical needs while you make decisions.

  • They can: link you to medical care; help replace a lost or stolen passport; contact family or your employer (with your consent); provide lists of lawyers; explain possible financial assistance to return to your country; and request updates from local authorities on your case; file a report on your behalf after you have safely left the country.
  • They cannot: investigate crimes, give legal advice or represent you, serve as official interpreters, or pay legal or medical costs.
  • How to find yours: Go to Embassy or Consulate, select your country of citizenship, and use the “Find embassy / consulate” link to open the directory for the country you’re currently in.

Information here is general and may change or be inaccurate depending on your country of citizenship—confirm details with the local embassy or consulate before you act.
